# Conditionals

## The 5 Conditionals at a Glance

| Conditional | Time Frame                 | Reality / Possibility           | Formula                                                       |
| ----------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Zero        | General / Always           | 100% Real (Facts/Truths)        | `If + Present Simple, ... Present Simple`                     |
| First       | Future                     | Real / High Probability         | `If + Present Simple, ... Will + Verb`                        |
| Second      | Present / Future           | Unreal / Imaginary / Impossible | `If + Past Simple, ... Would + Verb`                          |
| Third       | Past                       | Unreal / Impossible (Too late)  | `If + Past Perfect, ... Would Have + Past Participle`         |
| Mixed (4th) | Past $\rightarrow$ Present | Changing past changes present   | `If + Past Perfect, ... Would + Verb` _(Most common variant)_ |

## 0. The Zero Conditional (General Truths)

We use the zero conditional to talk about things that are always true, like scientific facts, general laws, or habits. There is no uncertainty here.

- Structure: `If/When + Present Simple, ... Present Simple`
- Example: "If you heat ice, it melts."
- Example: "When I stay up late, I feel tired the next day."

## 1. The First Conditional (Real Future Possibilities)

We use the first conditional for a specific future situation and its highly likely result. It is realistic and possible.

- Structure: `If + Present Simple, ... Will + Base Verb`
- Example: "If it rains tomorrow, I will bring an umbrella."
- Example: "If you study for the test, you will pass."

## 2. The Second Conditional (Imaginary / Hypothetical Present or Future)

We use the second conditional to talk about unreal, imaginary, or highly unlikely situations in the present or future. It’s the "dreaming" conditional.

- Structure: `If + Past Simple, ... Would + Base Verb`
- Example: "If I won the lottery, I would buy a boat." _(Unlikely to happen)_
- Example: "If I were you, I would take that job." _(I am not you, so it's imaginary)_
- _Note: In formal English, "were" is used instead of "was" for all subjects (If I were, If he were)._

## 3. The Third Conditional (Imaginary Past)

We use the third conditional to talk about the past. It describes an excuse, a regret, or a hypothetical past event that did not happen, meaning the result is now completely impossible.

- Structure: `If + Past Perfect, ... Would Have + Past Participle`
- Example: "If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam." _(Reality: I didn't study, and I failed)._
- Example: "If we had left earlier, we wouldn't have missed the train."

## 4. The Mixed Conditional (Past Cause, Present Effect)

While officially there are four standard types (0-3), standard grammar often refers to combinations of them as "Mixed Conditionals" (sometimes informally dubbed the 4th conditional).

The most common mixed conditional connects an unreal past condition with an unreal present result.

- Structure: `If + Past Perfect, ... Would + Base Verb`
- Example: "If I had been born in France, I would speak French fluently now."
- _The cause is in the past (born in France), but the result affects my present reality (speaking French now)._

- Example: "If I hadn't spent all my money yesterday, I would go to the movies with you tonight."
